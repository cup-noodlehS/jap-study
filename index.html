Pronunciation quirks for time (worth memorizing)

  • 4 o'clock = yoji (not shiji)
  • 7 o'clock = shichiji (not nanaji)
  • 9 o'clock = kuji (not kyuuji)
  • Minutes: 1, 3, 4, 6, 8, 10 become ~pun → ippun, sanpun, yonpun, roppun, happun/hachifun, juppun
  • Minutes: 2, 5, 7, 9 use ~fun → nifun, gofun, nanafun, kyuufun
Grammar

Patterns to master

1
Telling the time

Use ima (now) + the time + desu.

Ima ~ji ~fun desu.
Ima shichiji jippun desu. tap
It's 7:10 now.
Ima gogo sanji han desu. tap
It's 3:30 p.m. now.
2
The four forms of -masu verbs

Every -masu verb has four basic forms: present/future affirmative and negative, and past affirmative and negative. This is the engine of basic Japanese.

FormEndingExample (okimasu — to get up)Meaning
Present +-masuokimasuget up / will get up
Present −-masenokimasendon't get up / won't get up
Past +-mashitaokimashitagot up
Past −-masen deshitaokimasen deshitadidn't get up
3
Doing something at a specific time

Numerical/clock times take the particle ni. Relative time words (kyou, ashita, kesa, maiasa…) do NOT take ni.

Time ni Verb-masu
Watashi wa rokuji ni okimasu. tap
I get up at 6 o'clock.
Kyou benkyou shimasu. tap
I'll study today. (No "ni" — "kyou" is relative.)
Nichiyoubi ni tomodachi to aimasu. tap
I'll meet a friend on Sunday. (Days of the week can take ni, optionally.)
4
From X to Y

Mark a starting point with kara and an end point with made. Works for time, dates, and places.

N1 kara N2 made
Ginkou wa kuji kara sanji made desu. tap
The bank is open from 9 to 3.
Mainichi kuji kara gogo rokuji made hatarakimasu. tap
I work from 9 to 6 every day.
5
N1 and N2 (exhaustive list)

Use to to connect two (or more) nouns — like English "and" between things. Used when listing items, not actions.

N1 to N2
Ginkou no yasumi wa doyoubi to nichiyoubi desu. tap
The bank is closed on Saturday and Sunday.
6
Soudesu ka (Acknowledgment)

A polite "Oh, I see." or "Is that so?" used as a backchannel response when someone tells you something new. Not a real question.

A: Ashita yasumi desu. / B: Soudesu ka. tap
A: Tomorrow is a holiday. / B: Oh, is that so.

Grammar

Patterns to master

1
Going / coming / returning to a place

For movement to a place, mark the destination with e (written へ, pronounced "e"). Used only with motion verbs: ikimasu, kimasu, kaerimasu.

Place e ikimasu / kimasu / kaerimasu
Watashi wa Kyouto e ikimasu. tap
I'm going to Kyoto.
Ashita doko e mo ikimasen. tap
Tomorrow I'm not going anywhere. ("doko e mo" + negative = nowhere)
Raishuu kuni e kaerimasu. tap
I'll return to my country next week.
2
By what means of transportation

Use de to mark the vehicle/tool you use. Exception: walking uses aruite (no particle).

Vehicle de ikimasu
Densha de ikimasu. tap
I'll go by train.
Aruite kaerimasu. tap
I'll walk home. (No "de" for walking!)
Eki kara basu de kimashita. tap
I came from the station by bus.
3
Doing something with someone

Use to to mark the person you do something with. For doing something alone, use hitori de.

Person to Verb
Kazoku to Nihon e kimashita. tap
I came to Japan with my family.
Hitori de Tokyo e ikimasu. tap
I'll go to Tokyo alone.
4
When? — itsu

itsu means "when" and does NOT take ni. Use it like any other time word at the front of the question.

Itsu Nihon e kimashita ka. tap
When did you come to Japan?
Tanjoubi wa itsu desu ka. tap
When is your birthday?

Grammar

Patterns to master

1
Doing something to an object

The particle o (written を, pronounced "o") marks the direct object — the thing the verb acts on.

Object o Verb-masu
Pan o tabemasu. tap
I eat bread.
Ocha o nomimasu. tap
I drink tea.
Mainichi shinbun o yomimasu. tap
I read the newspaper every day.
2
"Doing N" — N o shimasu

Many nouns (sports, study tasks, events) take shimasu with o to mean "do N."

Tenisu o shimasu. tap
I play tennis.
Konban shukudai o shimasu. tap
I'll do homework tonight.
3
"What will you do?" — nani o ~
Time nani o shimasu ka.
Nichiyoubi nani o shimasu ka. tap
What will you do on Sunday?
Asa nani o tabemasu ka. tap
What do you eat in the morning?
4
"Something" — nanika

In a question, nanika means "anything/something." When asking, the o is usually dropped.

Asa nanika tabemasu ka. tap
Do you eat anything in the morning?
Hai, pan o tabemasu. / Iie, nani mo tabemasen. tap
Yes, I eat bread. / No, I don't eat anything. ("nani mo" + negative = nothing)
5
Where you do something

This is the big one. Use de to mark the place where an action happens. Don't confuse this with e (destination) or ni (existence/time).

Place de Object o Verb
Resutoran de hirugohan o tabemasu. tap
I eat lunch at the restaurant.
Toshokan de benkyou shimasu. tap
I study at the library.
Niwa de shashin o torimashita. tap
I took a photo in the garden.
6
Inviting — "Won't you ~?"

Use the negative -masen ka to invite someone politely. This isn't a real "no" question — it's a friendly invitation.

(Issho ni) Verb-masen ka.
Issho ni hirugohan o tabemasen ka. tap
Won't you have lunch with me? / Wanna grab lunch together?
Ashita Kyouto e ikimasen ka. tap
Won't you go to Kyoto tomorrow?
7
"Let's ~" — -mashou

A more positive proposal/agreement than -masen ka. Often the response to an invitation.

Verb-mashou.
A: Issho ni biiru o nomimasen ka. / B: Ee, nomimashou. tap
A: Want to have a beer together? / B: Yes, let's!
Soredewa, juuniji ni eki de aimashou. tap
Well then, let's meet at the station at 12.

The Particle Cheat Sheet

If you confuse ni / e / de, you'll get tripped up across these whole three chapters. Memorize this table.

ni
Point in timespecific clock/calendar times
shichiji ni okimasu — get up at 7
e
Destination (movement)where you're going to
gakkou e ikimasu — go to school
de
Location of an action / meanswhere it happens / what tool
resutoran de tabemasu — eat at the restaurant
basu de ikimasu — go by bus
o
Direct objectthe thing acted upon
pan o tabemasu — eat bread
to
"And" / "With"list of nouns, or companion
tomodachi to ikimasu — go with a friend
kara
Starting pointfrom a time or place
kuji kara — from 9 o'clock
made
End pointuntil a time or place
sanji made — until 3 o'clock
mo
"Also" / "(not) any"with negatives forms "no…"
nani mo tabemasen — don't eat anything

How to actually retain this

  • Don't memorize particles by name — memorize them by role. Ask "what job is this particle doing?" not "which particle goes here?"
  • The ni / e / de triangle is the heart of these chapters. Practice with the same sentence: "I [verb] at 7 / to school / on foot." Each one is a different particle.
  • Make a chart of your daily routine in Japanese. "6-ji ni okimasu. 7-ji ni asagohan o tabemasu. 9-ji kara gogo 5-ji made hatarakimasu." This single exercise uses 80% of these chapters.
  • For invitations, learn the rhythm: Q: ~masen ka. → A: Ee, ~mashou. It's almost ritual.
  • Drill the irregular numbers (yoji, shichiji, kuji; tsuitachi, futsuka, mikka…). They show up in every exam.
